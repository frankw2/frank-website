James Michaelson MGH Cost Analysis

Understanding the costs related to non-small lung cancer (NSCLC) patients.

TLDR;

NSCLC patients cost MGH $58 million/year. Want to uncover high, unnecessary costs.

Their high level approach is:

  1. Collect and consolidate data
  2. Identify appropriate subsets of data
  3. Identify medical practices that
    1. Don’t improve survival
    2. Save maximum $$ if average == median cost

They found:

  • Baseline survival rates
  • Costs are right skewed and idependent of disease attributes
  • The high costs are attributed to a particular service(chemo+radiation)
  • High chemo costs != better survival rates
  • Reducing chemo costs most reduces total costs

  • Manually restricted attributes (columns) to consider to service types (chemo, surgery, etc) and doctors

Datasets

The dataset joins three base tables, they then restricted analyses to subsets of the joined dataset based on what they wanted.

Base Datasets

TSI Billing database

  • Oct 2005 to Dec 2010
  • 3839 patients
  • Prefiltered for only cancer related MGH bills (how filter was done is unknown)
  • Patients with billing records
    • Over all cancer: billing info >94% 2007 and onwards
    • Over NSCLC: > 80% 2006 and onwards

    • 10% first billed after 60 months

  • % of records for top types

    • Radiation 27.4%
    • Chemo Infusion 15%

Tumor Registry

  • ICD-O-3 codes to tell if NSCLC
  • 12,361 of 17,025 are NSCLC

Research Patient Data Registry

  • These are pathology, encounter etc reports
  • 90% of patients 1990-94. 100% onwards
  • # records for top types

Join between the datasets by medical record number (MRN)

Statistics

  • 12,361 patients since 1960

Picking Data-subsets

The analysis constructed 10 datasets (A-G) that varied the following variables (this doesn’t really matter, included for reference):

  • All billing or any
  • All cancer, Lung, or NSCLC
  • All vs Surgical vs nonsurgical
  • Only single lung cancer, or multiple/recurrances allowed
  • W/wout mediastinoscopy
  • All vs chemo only with chema bills
  • Treated + diagnosed @ MGH

Analyses

They performed a multi-step analysis by first finding overall costs, then analyzing costs by service and identifying chemo as an outlier, then analyzing why chemo is expensive.

Initial Stats

First understand survival/mortality rates of all lung cancer patients

  • 2.5 years median survival
  • 4.3 years average survival
  • No change in death rates 5, 10 years post diagnosis
  • 1 year has reduced, most likely due to lead time bias

Then compared Kaplan-Meier mortality rates in each of the datasets:

  • X-axis: time after diagnosis
  • Y-axis: mortality
  • Trends are very similar in all of the datasets

Conclusion: Don’t smoke. Lung cancer sucks. Everyone dies.

First Analysis (overall costs)

Dataset: NSCLC patients with billing info, single lung cancer (no recurrance), billing filtered by diagnosis=“lung neoplasm” and between 1st diagnosis and 5yrs after

  • Costs for each type of service right skewed (avg >> median)
    • 100th percentile $70k/patient vs 90th percentile $34.4k/patient
  • Cost (decile) indep. of mortality, cancer stage, tumor size, year of diagnosis, etc

Mediastinoscopy usage vs Survival (not clear why this was analyzed)

  • Equal treatments across ages and cancer stages
  • Increased use based on tumor size (≥40) –> higher mortality with treatment
  • Strangely higher mortality for patients undergoing mediastinoscopy with tumor sizes of 10-20mm

Second Analysis (by service)

Dataset: Removed diagnosis=“lung neoplasm” filter. Turns out many NSCLC-related bills are not correctly labelled. This analysis is “more comprehensive”.

Costs and revenue by service

  • Total cost $56M ($40k/patient)
    • Clinical visits -$1.5k/patient
    • Medical -$2.2k/patient
  • Total revenue $69M ($49k/patient)
    • Chemo $10k/patient
    • Radiation $19k/patient
  • Total margin $18M ($12.8k/patient)
  • Later cost analysis is focused on just costs, not revenue (based on capitization assumption?)

Chemo is most expensive

  • 79% of total costs
  • Chemo cost is independent of cancer stage and mortality

3yr patient costs w/ and w/out systemic treatment (chemo)

  • chemo: $21k vs $6.2k
  • overall $30k vs $22k

Radiation is also expensive

  • Intensity modulated radiation therapy (IMRT) is 2X other types of radiation treatment
    • 70% of radiation treatments are IMRT
    • extra $7.6k/patient –> $1.8M/year
  • Overall, 16% of all lung cancer patients get IMRT

Third Analysis (thorough analysis)

Dataset: restricted to NSCLC patients that have all bill records and fully diagnosed and treated at MGH. Analyzed patients that got chemo and that got surgery.

  • 12% of all records of NSCLC patients at MGH between Oct 05 - Dec 11
  • 3.8% of all lung cancer patients in tumor registry
  • avg treatment length is 20.2 months
  • cost/patient/year:
    • $62k for dead
    • $20k for alive
    • $34k average
  • $34k * 1700 patients/year = $58M/year
  • multiply per-patient cost by 1010x to get extrapolated MGH costs

Largest costs are

  • Chemo:
    • 31% of all patients
    • 40% non surgery
    • 24% surgery
  • Surgery
    • 24% overall
    • 43% of patients w/surgery
  • Diagnostics, labs, and inpatient medical are very right skewed but no analysis.
    • Inpatient costs are nonnegligable (presumably for surgical patients)
    • Chemo + inpatient costs:
      • $55k/patient average
      • $24k/patient median
      • $34M/year extra costs (out of $58M/year)

What if avg(cost) == median(cost)?

  • Chemo: $58k -> $40k * 1010 = $18M
  • Surgery: negligable (0.6% of total costs)
  • no analysis for other costs

Does it impact mortality rates? No.

Diving Deep Analysis (why is chemo so expensive?)

Order chemo costs by doctor and compare max and median costs. Perform similar analysis on baseline of surgeons.

Medical Oncologist

  • min: $2.8k, max: $33k/patient
  • cost/infusion indep. of # infusions (avg vs count)
  • total cost linear corr. with # infusions
  • 10-15% patients infused w/in 1 month of death (no need)

References

Kaplan-Meier

Given initial population N0, number of surviving at time i, Si, and number still in population in previous time i-1, Ni-1, the probability of survival to time i was:

P(s,i) = Si / Ni-1

Probability of dying between times i-1 and i is:

P(d,i) = 1 - P(s,i)

Probability of survival until time i is

1 - Πi P(d,i)

The curve is a step function.
